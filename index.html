Data Quality Hub

An open resource for comparing data quality across online survey platforms. Researchers submit their study results, and the dashboard aggregates all submissions to reveal platform-level insights.

What is "data quality"?

Data quality in online surveys refers to how reliably participants engage with study materials. Common checks include:

  • Attention checks — questions designed to verify participants are reading carefully
  • AI/bot detection — identifying responses generated by language models rather than humans
  • Account fraud detection — flagging duplicate accounts, VPN usage, or suspicious sign-up patterns
  • Typing analysis — distinguishing typed responses from pasted text; measuring typing speed

Why Data Quality Hub?

The collection of high-quality data is essential to social science research. Online surveys have become a common source of data on human behavior, offering larger and more diverse samples at lower costs. Yet this raises a central concern: whether a given response reflects the decision of an attentive human participant. This concern has intensified with the rise of AI agents that can emulate human participants, tools that assist in answering survey questions, and account fraud.

Data Quality Hub is an open-source community resource envisioned to track data quality trends in this evolving landscape. Rather than relying on individual researcher priors, this hub gathers empirical evidence across platforms — helping the research community make informed decisions about where and how to collect high-quality data.

Share Your Results →

What to share: Fill out a short Google Form with your study's data quality results — platform used, sample size, study date, overall pass rate, and optionally specific metrics like attention check rates, AI detection rates, or account fraud rates.

Format: All fields are straightforward (names, percentages, short descriptions). The form takes about 2–3 minutes to complete. You can submit multiple entries if your study covers several platforms or uses a two-stage design.

What happens next: Submissions are reviewed before appearing on the dashboard. Once approved, your data points will show up in the trend charts and studies table, contributing to the community's understanding of platform-level data quality.
